## Project One-Liner

Sound Drop is an immersive hand-tracking sound editing prototype paired with a research-style exploration of how sound can be captured, visualized, annotated, and retrieved through spatial interaction.

## Context

Confirmed: Sound Drop appears on the original site as an experience project with the tags Research Project, Experience Design, and Hand-Tracking. The site describes it as an immersive space that helps players edit sound more interactively and intuitively.

Confirmed from assets: the project includes a Unity-style spatial interface, an audio library with waveform thumbnails, hand/gesture-based interaction, sound-recording experiments with physical objects, sound-field visualizations, annotation grids, clustering comparisons, accuracy charts, and a user testing photo.

Inferred: This project should be presented as two connected layers, not just one app screen. One layer is the experiential prototype: manipulating sound objects in space. The other layer is the research/analysis foundation: understanding how sound events can be represented, labeled, and found.

## Design Problem

Confirmed: The original project explored a more interactive and intuitive way to edit sound in an immersive space.

Inferred: The design problem was to move sound editing away from flat timeline controls and toward physical/spatial manipulation. To make that credible, the project also needed a way to organize sound material: capture samples, compare acoustic features, visualize intensity, annotate examples, and evaluate retrieval or classification approaches.

## Audience / Use Case

Primary audience: people experimenting with sound design, spatial audio, or creative audio editing.

Use case: a user enters a virtual/immersive scene, selects or places sound objects, edits their spatial properties with hand-tracking or direct gestures, and uses a structured sound library or analysis system to choose appropriate clips.

Secondary use case, inferred from charts and experiment boards: research viewers evaluating whether different representations of sound help with annotation, retrieval, or recognition.

## System / Gameplay / Interaction Model

Confirmed visual model:

1. Sound assets are represented as waveform thumbnails in a library-like panel.
2. Users appear to add or assign sounds to objects or positions in an immersive scene.
3. The Unity editor/prototype view shows 3D primitives, audio source settings, and spatial placement.
4. The research boards analyze sound events through object material, shape, action, annotation, and visualized intensity.

Inferred interaction loop:

1. Choose or record a sound.
2. Place it into a spatial environment.
3. Manipulate the sound object through hand-tracking or direct gestures.
4. Listen, adjust, and organize the sound through visual cues.
5. Use annotation/retrieval logic to compare or select sounds more efficiently.

## Process Story

The project appears to start with a prototype goal: make sound editing feel embodied and spatial. The first visual layer shows a dark immersive environment with floating geometric objects and a user-facing interface. The next layer moves into implementation evidence: Unity, code, audio source settings, and a waveform-based library.

The page then shifts from interface to research method. The experiment board shows three sets of physical objects made of different materials and shapes. The text on the board says the process began with three different sets of objects, then tapped them with a plastic stick and recorded the sound, and finally dropped each object from a height onto a wooden surface and recorded the sound. The later boards compare annotations, sound intensity levels, clusters, and accuracy. This suggests the old Wix page wanted viewers to understand both the poetic interaction concept and the technical/research backbone behind it.
