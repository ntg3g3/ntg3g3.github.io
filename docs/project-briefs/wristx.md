## Project One-Liner

WristX is a wearable rehabilitation product-system concept that combines a wrist/finger device, mobile app, and playful exercise flow to support people at risk of or recovering from carpal tunnel syndrome.

## Context

Confirmed: WristX is listed as an Individual Project with System Design and 3D Modeling tags. The site describes it as a system/product to assist rehabilitation for people with Carpel-tunnel Syndrome.

Confirmed from assets: the project includes a phone app paired with a hand-worn device, rehabilitation exercise research, occupational risk data, existing-solution comparison, mood board, sketches, 3D renders, final design boards, and final specs.

Inferred: The project frames rehabilitation as both a health behavior and an interaction design problem. It tries to make exercises more guided, trackable, and playful, using the phone as the instruction/progress layer and the wearable as the sensing/control layer.

## Design Problem

Confirmed: The product supports rehabilitation for carpal tunnel syndrome.

Inferred: The design problem is that wrist/finger rehabilitation is repetitive, easy to neglect, and often disconnected from feedback. WristX appears to ask how a device/app system can guide correct motion, make exercises more engaging, and help users build a routine without turning the product into a purely medical-looking brace.

## Audience / Use Case

Primary audience: people who experience wrist strain or carpal tunnel symptoms, especially people in occupations with repetitive hand/wrist use.

Confirmed/inferred user groups from boards: office/computer users, telecommunications or assembly workers, software publishers, and other occupations listed in the CTS risk board.

Use case: the user wears or attaches a hand/wrist device, opens the app, follows exercise or game-like tasks, receives reminders/progress feedback, and uses the device as a controller or motion guide for rehabilitation movements.

## System / Gameplay / Interaction Model

Confirmed visual model:

1. The final system pairs a phone interface with a hand/wrist-mounted device.
2. The phone app shows remaining exercise time, live device connection, and game-like exercise options.
3. The device appears to connect fingers to a wrist module through tension lines or sensors.
4. Research boards show exercises including active range of motion, tendon glides, wrist stretch, finger exercise, and strengthening.
5. The flow includes reminders, training/exercise, and feedback or progress views.

Inferred interaction loop:

1. User receives reminder or chooses a rehab session.
2. User connects or wears the device.
3. App guides exercise or a playful mini-game controlled by finger/wrist movement.
4. Device tracks movement or creates resistance/feedback.
5. User sees progress, completion, or comparison over time.
