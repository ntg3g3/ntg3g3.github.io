## Project One-Liner

Roominate is a co-living IoT service concept that uses a mobile app, shared-space hardware cues, and lightweight data/reporting to help roommates coordinate chores and manage common spaces.

## Context

Confirmed: Roominate is listed as an Experience Design, System Design, and Internet of Things project. The original site describes it as a co-living system for tracking chores and managing shared space more efficiently.

Confirmed from assets: the project includes mobile app screens and animations, roommate profiles, stakeholder mapping, research notes about sensitivity to data collection, survey/data charts, service blueprint/system map, scenario illustrations in shared rooms, hardware renders with triangular modules, app user flow, and user testing/presentation photos.

Inferred: Roominate was not only a chore tracker. It appears to address the emotional and social friction of shared living: privacy, responsibility, tolerance, evidence, reminders, and roommate feedback.

## Design Problem

Confirmed: The project aims to help people in co-living situations track chores and manage shared spaces.

Inferred from research notes and system maps: the design problem was to create accountability without making roommates feel surveilled. The notes explicitly discuss sensitivity to data collection, local versus cloud storage, face/fingerprint/voice/gait/action-at-home data, and privacy concerns. The concept seems to search for a tolerable middle ground: enough evidence to reduce conflict, but not so much monitoring that it becomes invasive.

## Audience / Use Case

Primary audience: roommates or co-living residents sharing kitchens, living rooms, bathrooms, or other common spaces.

Use case: a roommate receives or confirms a chore through the app or a shared physical device, performs a task such as cleaning a living room, gets feedback through light/app notification, and later communicates or reviews shared-space status/data with roommates.

Secondary users: property managers or co-living organizers may be implied by stakeholder maps, but this is not confirmed.

## System / Gameplay / Interaction Model

Confirmed visual model:

1. Mobile app: profile screen, onboarding/welcome, device/control screen, chore or shared-space screens.
2. Hardware: triangular base and detachable/pressable modules with light feedback.
3. Shared-room scenarios: annotated room images showing generated life patterns, schedules, stress/break information, and shared-space activity.
4. Service blueprint: user goes to cleaner, presses confirm button, cleans living room, confirms name in app, triggers lighting, communicates in app, and reports on data.
5. Feedback channels: touching feedback, sound feedback, app notification, connected lighting, roommate feedback, and reports.

Inferred interaction loop:

1. A shared-space task or issue is detected, scheduled, or manually entered.
2. Roommates receive a prompt through app, light, sound, or physical object.
3. A person confirms and completes a chore.
4. The system provides visual feedback and records lightweight evidence.
5. Roommates use app communication or reports to resolve responsibility and maintain shared-space expectations.

## Original Wix Page Logic

Confirmed from the contact sheet: the old Wix page has 39 assets and mixes mobile GIFs, research artifacts, stakeholder diagrams, service flow maps, scenario renderings, hardware renders, data charts, app screens, user testing photos, and final logos/renders. Unlike the smaller projects, Roominate's page relies heavily on motion assets and system diagrams.

Inferred layout logic: the old page was structured like a service-design case study with minimal explanatory text. The images are meant to do the thinking:

1. App/brand opening to establish the product identity.
2. Research evidence: notes, stakeholders, post-its, survey/data charts.
3. Scenario images showing how data and chores appear inside a shared home.
4. Service blueprint and app user flow to explain the system.
5. Mobile interaction loops and static screens to show behavior over time.
6. Physical IoT object renders and in-room cues to show the non-screen part of the system.
7. User testing/presentation photos as closing evidence.

The final page should keep Roominate more visual and modular than text-heavy. The service blueprint and scenario images should be large, while app GIFs can be shown as a controlled row/grid. Research notes about privacy should not be hidden: they explain why the system is designed around lightweight evidence rather than full surveillance.
